## Part 1: The Big Picture (3 min)

> Here's what Nucleus Phone actually is. It's a dialer that sits on top of HubSpot and does three things no other dialer does.
>
> One, it researches every contact before you call them. You never dial cold. By the time you click Call, you know their name, their shop, what equipment they probably run, what problems they're dealing with, and which of our products fits.
>
> Two, it coaches you while you're on the phone. That's the Reactor. It listens to the conversation, detects what equipment they mention, sizes a system in real time, and feeds you suggestions. We'll spend most of our time here because this is the thing that changes the game.
>
> Three, it handles all the after-call work. Recording, transcription, HubSpot logging, Slack alerts. You hang up, tag the outcome, and move on. No CRM data entry.

## Part 4: The Reactor — This Is the Difference Maker (8 min)

> OK. The Reactor is the center of the cockpit and it's the reason this system is different from every other dialer. It has two halves that work together while you're on a live call. Let me walk you through both.

### 4a: Live Analysis — Equipment Detection + Sizing

> When you're on a call and the prospect mentions equipment, the Reactor picks it up in real time.
>
> Say Mike tells you he's running a 20 year old Ingersoll Rand piston unit that just died. A badge pops up: "Ingersoll Rand, piston, detected." He mentions he's got five CNC machines running at 125 PSI. The demand bar updates: total CFM, peak demand, pressure requirement.
>
> Then the magic happens. The Reactor sizes a replacement system. It pulls from our catalog and shows you: "JRS-10E, 10 HP rotary screw, 42 CFM at 125 PSI, $9,500." Plus the dryer, filters, and oil/water separator that complete the system.
>
> You don't need to know any of this going in. The Reactor builds the recommendation while Mike is talking. All you do is relay it naturally. "Based on what you're telling me, here's what fits your shop."
>
> It'll also flag alternatives. If a VSD drive unit would save him energy, it says so. If he needs a desiccant dryer instead of a refrigerated dryer because of AS9100 certification, the Reactor knows.

### 4b: Conversation Navigator — Real-Time Coaching

> The second half is the Navigator. It sits below Live Analysis and handles the human side of the call.
>
> **Sentiment Arc.** This is a line graph that tracks how the conversation is going emotionally. Green above the line means positive, red below means negative. You can see at a glance if the prospect is warming up or cooling off. It also tells you the momentum. "Building" means you're heading the right direction. "Declining" means pull back, ask a question, let them talk.
>
> **Phase tracking.** The Navigator knows where you are in the conversation. Greeting, discovery, qualification, equipment discussion, objection handling, pricing, closing. It shows the current phase so you can see if you're rushing to product too early or spending too long on small talk.
>
> **Suggestion cards.** These pop up during the call with things to say. They come from different sources: AI predictions based on the discovery phase, phase-specific response banks, real-time Claude Haiku suggestions, and objection rebuttals. They auto-dismiss after 30 seconds so they don't pile up. You don't have to use them. Think of them like a spotter at the gym. They're there if you need a nudge.
>
> **The key thing to understand about the Reactor:** it's not telling you what to say. It's giving you information and options so YOU can have a natural conversation. The best callers glance at the Reactor for intel and then talk like a human being. They don't read suggestion cards out loud.

## Part 6: After the Call — Disposition (2 min)

> When you hang up, disposition screen pops up. Takes 15 seconds, do not skip it.
>
> Tag the outcome: Connected, Voicemail, No Answer, Callback, Gatekeeper, Wrong Number, Not Interested.
>
> If you connected: qualify them. Hot means they want a quote or next step. Warm means interested, needs follow up. Cold means polite but not now.
>
> Drop a quick note. Doesn't need to be long. "Runs three Haas CNC, compressor died last week, wants a quote on JRS-10E + dryer. Sending specs tonight." That note goes straight into HubSpot and Slack.
>
> Click Save, you're back to contacts, ready for the next one.

## Part 7: Practice Mode — Mike Garza Simulator (5 min)

> Before you make your first real call, you're going to practice. Click Practice in the nav.
>
> This puts you on a call with an AI version of Mike Garza. He's a real character based on a real customer profile. Three difficulty levels.
>
> **Easy Mike** is friendly. He's got a compressor emergency and he wants help. Good for learning the cockpit flow without pressure.
>
> **Medium Mike** is skeptical. He's talked to three reps this week and he's tired of sales pitches. You have to earn his attention.
>
> **Hard Mike** is hostile. Bad day, doesn't want to be on the phone, challenges everything you say. This is for sharpening objection handling after you've got the basics down.
>
> The Reactor works in practice mode too. Same equipment detection, same sentiment arc, same suggestions. So you're training with the full cockpit experience.
>
> After the call, you get an AI scorecard. Five categories: Rapport, Discovery, Objection Handling, Product Knowledge, and Close. Each scored 0 to 10 with specific feedback on what worked and what to improve.
>
> **Do two practice runs on Easy before your first real call.** Get comfortable with where everything is on screen. After that, try Medium. We'll check your scores and talk about what to adjust.

> A few ground rules.
>
> Every call gets dispositioned. No exceptions. The data feeds the pipeline and the team's visibility.
>
> Hot leads get a Slack alert automatically. If you surface something hot and Alex or I aren't responding in Slack, text me directly.
>
> You don't need to close deals. Your job is to qualify. Have the conversation, find the need, tag it warm or hot, and move on. Alex handles the close on anything you surface that needs a deeper technical conversation.
>
> If you're unsure about anything on a call, it's always OK to say "let me get you the exact specs on that and follow up by email." Nobody expects you to have every answer on day one. The cockpit has 90% of what you need, and for the other 10%, you ask the team.
